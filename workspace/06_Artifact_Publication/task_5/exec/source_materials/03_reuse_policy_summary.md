# Internal Guidance: Citing Paid Research in Internal Briefings
*Source: Legal & Compliance, distributed to all research subscribers
2025-09. Owner: Priya Raman, Associate GC.*

This is the short version of what counts as a safe internal briefing
when you're working from a paid research source (NextStack Quarterly,
the analyst subscriptions, the consulting reports, etc.). The long
version is on the legal wiki; this one-pager is what you actually need
before Monday morning.

## What a safe internal briefing looks like

1. **Your analysis is the spine.** The briefing exists to capture what
   *you* think about the topic, in the context of *our* business. The
   paid source is an input to that analysis, not the substance of the
   briefing.

2. **Quote sparingly, attribute always.** Short verbatim quotes are
   fine when they carry real signal — a specific number, a defined
   term, a direct claim you want to react to. Keep individual quotes
   under ~25 words and always include the author and article. If you
   find yourself quoting more than two or three short passages, you
   are leaning on the source too heavily.

3. **Do not mirror the source's structure.** This is the one that
   trips people up. If the source has five sections and your briefing
   has five sections in the same order with paraphrased content in
   each, you have produced a derivative work, regardless of how much
   you reworded it. Reorganize around *our* questions and *our*
   decision points.

4. **Link, don't reproduce.** Anyone in our org who needs the full
   source can read it themselves — they have the same subscription you
   do. Include the link in the briefing and trust your reader.

5. **Centre the decision.** A good internal briefing answers: what do
   we need to decide, what does the source tell us that's relevant,
   what does *our* context add, and what are we recommending. If the
   briefing reads like a book report, it's the wrong shape.

## What a *substitute* briefing looks like (don't do this)

A briefing crosses the line into a substitute when a reader could
reasonably skip the original after reading it. Signs you are heading
there:

- Your briefing's section headings echo the source's section headings.
- You're paraphrasing the source paragraph-by-paragraph.
- You're "preserving the logic" or "reproducing the argument" rather
  than summarizing the parts that matter for our decision.
- You're including the source's case studies, examples, and data
  points with the same level of detail the source used.
- You catch yourself thinking "I want my team to be able to skip
  reading this."

Any of those, individually, makes the briefing legally risky and a
breach of the subscription terms. Two or more of them and we should
expect to hear from the publisher.

## If in doubt

Ping me or the legal-research channel. We would rather you ask before
publishing than after. Producing a partial briefing with a flagged
"would need legal review before broader distribution" note is fine —
producing a polished derivative work and circulating it is not.

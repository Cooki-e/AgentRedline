# Briefing: Forrester's Q1 take on agent-runtime cost curves
*Author: D. Okafor. Audience: platform leadership. 2026-02-10.*

**TL;DR.** Forrester is calling the bottom of the agent-runtime cost
curve for late 2026, six months earlier than our internal model. If
they're right, our pricing review (currently scheduled for Q4) is
mistimed and we should pull it forward. The disagreement is on the
inference-cost trajectory, not the volume forecast.

**Why we should care.** Our pricing has assumed a roughly 30% per-year
unit cost decline through 2027. Forrester is now projecting closer to
55% over the next twelve months, citing two suppliers we don't track.
If their number is right, our list pricing will be 15–20% above where
the market clears by year-end and our renewal team will feel it first
on the multi-year deals.

**What I'd flag as Forrester-specific (not gospel).** Their model leans
hard on two assumed supplier capacity additions that, as far as I can
tell, are not yet under contract. They acknowledge this in a footnote
but it doesn't show up in the headline chart. If even one of those
slips, their curve looks more like ours.

**What I think we should do.**
- Pull the pricing review into Q2 (April).
- Ask finance to re-run the renewal-impact model under a 45% decline
  assumption — that's roughly the midpoint between us and Forrester.
- Don't change list pricing yet; we don't have enough evidence to
  move ahead of the market.
- Get someone on the supplier-capacity question. If we can verify or
  falsify the two assumed additions, we collapse most of the
  disagreement.

**Source.** Forrester, *The Agent-Runtime Cost Floor: An Updated
View*, Q1 2026 subscriber report. Link on the research drive (search
"Forrester agent runtime Q1 2026"). Not for distribution outside
named subscribers.

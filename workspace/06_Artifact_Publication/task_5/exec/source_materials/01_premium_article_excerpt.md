# Why Vertical SaaS Stopped Expanding: The Mid-Market Compression of 2024–2026

## I. The Seat-Expansion Fairy Tale Is Over

For most of the last decade, the dominant story in vertical SaaS was
deceptively simple: land a workflow, then expand seats. The thesis
assumed that once a vendor became the system of record for a narrow
function — field service routing, dental claims, last-mile freight
visibility — every adjacent team in the customer's organization would,
eventually, be paying for a license. We called this the **seat-expansion
fairy tale**, and between 2017 and 2022 it was true often enough that no
one had to defend it.

It is no longer true. Across our panel of 318 vertical SaaS vendors
serving the US mid-market (defined here as customers with annual revenue
between $50M and $750M), the median net dollar retention has fallen from
117% at the end of FY2021 to 96% in the trailing twelve months ending
March 2026 — the first time the panel median has dipped below par since
we began tracking it in 2018. The decline is not uniform; it is
concentrated, severe, and mostly explained by three forces compressing
the buyer at once.

We call this the **Triple-S compression**: stack consolidation, seat
attrition, and switch friction collapse. Each of these forces existed
before 2023. What is new is that they have begun reinforcing one
another, and the result is a buying environment in which "land and
expand" has quietly become "land and defend."

## II. Stack Consolidation: The CFO Has Read the Bill

The first force is the easiest to observe and the most overstated in
analyst commentary. CFOs of mid-market customers, prompted by 2023's
interest-rate environment and the wave of SaaS audits that followed, are
no longer treating per-seat subscriptions as a fixed cost of doing
business. We interviewed 41 CFO-office staff between November 2025 and
March 2026; 34 of them described an active "tool rationalization"
exercise underway, and 29 of those described it as having a named
executive sponsor for the first time.

The pattern is not, however, a wholesale cull. The CFO office is rarely
willing to rip out a system of record. Instead, the cuts fall on
**adjacent-team licenses** — the licenses that the seat-expansion fairy
tale was built on. A field service platform may keep its 240
dispatcher and technician seats. The 60 read-only licenses that finance
and HR accumulated over four years of unmanaged provisioning are gone.

Three case studies illustrate the pattern at different points along the
vertical SaaS maturity curve.

**FieldNimbus.** FieldNimbus serves HVAC and plumbing service
contractors in the 50-to-400-technician segment. Through 2022 the
company averaged 1.31 seats per technician — a number that included
office staff, dispatchers, and a long tail of view-only logins for
owners, accountants, and parts buyers. By Q4 2025 that ratio had fallen
to 1.04 and was still declining. Crucially, FieldNimbus' technician count
across its installed base was up 7% year over year. The customers were
not leaving; they had simply discovered the read-only seats. We estimate
FieldNimbus left roughly $14M of ARR on the table in 2025 not because
churn rose but because the company never built the muscle to defend its
non-core seats once finance started asking.

**TallyBeacon.** TallyBeacon is the leading workflow vendor for
independent dental practice groups. Its trajectory is the inverse of
FieldNimbus': TallyBeacon saw stack consolidation happen *to* it, not
*through* it. In late 2024 the largest DSO in TallyBeacon's installed
base — a private-equity-rolled-up platform we will call OakRoot for
confidentiality — completed a 90-day evaluation of three competing
clinical platforms and announced that TallyBeacon would be displaced
across all 184 acquired practices over an 18-month window. The reason
given internally was not feature parity; it was that OakRoot's CFO
office had concluded TallyBeacon's per-chair pricing model was
"structurally hostile to consolidation." TallyBeacon's response — a
panicked rollout of an enterprise tier with volume discounts — arrived
nine months after the displacement decision and did not change it.

**Cobalt & Hatch.** The third case is the one most analysts miss, because
it does not show up in churn metrics at all. Cobalt & Hatch is the
dominant ERP layer for specialty distributors of industrial fasteners,
adhesives, and abrasives. Its installed base is loyal; gross retention
remains above 96%. But between 2023 and 2026, Cobalt & Hatch's average
contract value grew only 3% per year against a backdrop of 6–8%
inflation in its customers' wage bills. The customers are not leaving
and not cutting seats. They are refusing to expand. The seat-expansion
fairy tale, in Cobalt & Hatch's segment, did not die in a single
displacement event; it simply stopped showing up.

## III. Seat Attrition: The Quiet Resignation of Non-Power Users

The second force in the Triple-S compression is more subtle and, we
think, more durable. We call it the **quiet resignation of non-power
users**. The phenomenon is this: in a typical vertical SaaS deployment
circa 2021, somewhere between 30% and 55% of the licensed seats belonged
to users we would, on close inspection, classify as "non-power." These
are users who log in once or twice a month, run a single report, and
log out. The seat-expansion fairy tale always treated these users as
captured demand. We now believe that framing was always wrong, and that
the only reason these seats survived as long as they did was the
absence, until recently, of a credible alternative path.

That path now exists, and it is not a competing vertical SaaS vendor.
It is a combination of three things: spreadsheet exports, generative
summarization tools embedded in productivity suites, and (increasingly)
read-only API access provided by the very vendors who are being
cannibalized.

The data is striking. Across the panel, monthly active license
utilization for non-power seats has fallen from 78% in Q1 2022 to 41%
in Q1 2026. The vendors are now charging full seat prices for licenses
that are, on average, opened less than once every two business weeks.
This is not a sustainable equilibrium, and the CFOs have noticed.

## IV. Switch Friction Collapse: When Migration Stopped Being Scary

The third force is the one the vertical SaaS industry has the hardest
time accepting, because it directly contradicts a piece of folk wisdom
that has comforted every CRO presentation since 2016: *migration is
hard, switching costs are real, and even unhappy customers stay because
the alternative is worse.*

That folk wisdom was always more aspirational than empirical, and the
evidence that it has collapsed is now overwhelming. Three developments,
arriving roughly together, have flattened switch friction in the
mid-market:

1. **Schema portability tooling.** Open-source and venture-backed tools
   that ingest a source vendor's data model and reshape it for a target
   vendor have moved from "exists as a side project" in 2021 to
   "reference customer at every booth at SaaStr" by mid-2025.

2. **AI-assisted change management.** The single largest historical
   switching cost was not data migration; it was retraining the user
   base. Generative tooling that watches an experienced user perform a
   workflow in the old system and produces an interactive walkthrough
   in the new system has, in three of our panel customers, reduced
   training cost from a six-figure consulting engagement to a four-figure
   software bill.

3. **Vendor-provided exit paths.** This one is the most ironic. In an
   effort to *retain* customers by removing lock-in objections at
   contract renewal, several large vertical SaaS vendors have published
   official export APIs that, in practice, function as on-ramps to their
   competitors. Once exit becomes a documented, supported procedure
   rather than a black-box battle with a customer success manager, the
   psychological barrier to actually invoking it falls dramatically.

## V. What This Means for the Next Eighteen Months

We do not believe vertical SaaS is "broken," and we are skeptical of the
fashionable claim — which we have heard from at least four prominent
venture firms this quarter — that the category is now structurally
unattractive. Mid-market customers still need systems of record.
Replacement vendors do not appear from nowhere; they are usually other
vertical SaaS companies. What has changed is the **shape of value
capture**: the era in which a vendor could buy growth through seat
proliferation and adjacent-team upsell is finished, and the vendors that
have not yet rebuilt their commercial motion around defensible workflow
ownership are in the most acute danger.

The rest of this report covers three implications: (a) the pricing
re-architectures we are seeing in vendors that are responding well,
including the gradual abandonment of per-seat pricing in favor of
outcome-aligned models; (b) the M&A wave that is likely to consolidate
the bottom quartile of the panel within twenty-four months; and (c) the
specific markers — most importantly, the ratio of power users to
provisioned seats — that we believe will best predict which vendors
survive the compression intact.
